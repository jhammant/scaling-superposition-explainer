Why does scaling language models work so reliably?

The paper’s answer is surprisingly geometric: models represent more features than they have dimensions, so meanings live in superposition. When that superposition is strong, error is dominated by vector overlap noise — and that noise falls roughly like 1 / width.

The puzzle

Neural scaling laws say larger models get lower loss in clean power-law patterns. But why should such a simple curve emerge from messy language data and huge transformers?

2

The bottleneck

LLMs need to represent tens of thousands of tokens — and likely many more abstract concepts — inside hidden spaces with only thousands of dimensions.

3

The toy model

The authors adapt Anthropic’s toy model of superposition: sparse latent features are encoded into a lower-dimensional space and reconstructed through a learned matrix.

4

The regime split

Weak superposition drops rare features; strong superposition keeps many/all features but lets their vectors overlap. Those two worlds scale differently.

5

The LLM check

Output layers of OPT, GPT-2, Qwen2.5, and Pythia look like the strong-superposition regime, with overlap-driven loss close to inverse-width scaling.

The intuition

There are too many meanings and too little room.

If a model had one independent dimension per feature, life would be easy: each feature could get a clean axis. LLMs do not have that luxury. A 50k-token vocabulary may be projected through a few thousand hidden dimensions, and the true feature set is probably larger than the vocabulary.

Superposition is the workaround: instead of assigning one axis per concept, the model packs many concepts into shared directions. The price is interference. The paper argues that, in today’s LLMs, that interference is not a nuisance around the edges — it is a central scaling mechanism.

More vectors than dimensions. Most pairs are almost orthogonal, but not perfectly. The leftover squared overlaps create predictable reconstruction error.

Methods, translated

The toy model is a pressure chamber for representations.

x

Sparse feature data

Each input is a vector of n latent features. Feature i activates with probability pᵢ, so features can be uniform, exponential, power-law, or otherwise skewed.

W

Compressed representation

A learned matrix W ∈ ℝⁿˣᵐ maps those features into a hidden space with m ≪ n. Each row of W is the representation vector for one feature.

L

Reconstruction loss

The model encodes and decodes the input, then measures reconstruction loss. Imperfect representation becomes a clean object to study.

Translation: the toy model strips away transformer details so you can ask one question: how does loss change when a crowded representation space gets wider?

Two scaling stories

Weak superposition is data-dependent. Strong superposition is geometric.

The paper’s key contrast is not “superposition or no superposition.” It is weak versus strong superposition.

Weak superposition

Drop the tail

The model represents the most frequent features cleanly and ignores rarer ones. Loss depends heavily on the feature-frequency distribution. Power-law data can create power-law loss; other distributions need not.

Strong superposition

Pack everything

The model represents many or all features with nonzero overlaps. Loss mostly comes from squared vector overlaps, which shrink like 1 / m for high-dimensional geometry.

What the paper reports

Actual LLMs match the strong-superposition picture.

Model families

The authors analyze four open-source families: OPT, GPT-2, Qwen2.5, and Pythia, spanning roughly 100M to 70B parameters.

Where they look

They focus on the language-model head / output-layer representation vectors, where many token representations must fit inside the model dimension.

What they measure

They estimate representation overlap, vector norms, token frequencies, and evaluation loss to compare real models with toy-model predictions.

Main quantitative fit

The reported overlap-loss exponent is about 0.91, close to the theoretical inverse-width exponent of 1. Chinchilla-style scaling is also reported as consistent, around 0.88.

Implications and limits

What changes if this is right?

  • Scaling has a mechanistic story: bigger models reduce representation interference, not just memorize more or fit smoother functions.
  • Packing quality matters: architectures or training methods that reduce harmful overlap could improve performance at fixed compute.
  • Interpretability stays hard: concepts are not stored as clean independent neurons or axes; they are entangled in shared directions.
  • There may be a regime change: once representation is no longer the bottleneck, the clean inverse-width story may stop dominating.
  • It is not the whole LLM: the toy model isolates representation learning. Transformer-layer skills, data quality, optimization, and compute still matter.
