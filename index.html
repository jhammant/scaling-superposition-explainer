Why bigger language models get better so reliably

The short version: models pack more features than they have dimensions. That crowding creates predictable interference. Add width, reduce the interference, lower the loss.

The problem is geometric crowding.

Language has far more tokens, facts, and latent concepts than a model has independent internal directions. So models do not give every feature a private axis. They place many features in the same space, almost orthogonal but not perfectly separate.

📦

Superposition

Represent n features in only m dimensions. The vectors overlap slightly instead of being clean one-feature-per-axis slots.

〰️

Interference

Those tiny overlaps show up as noise in prediction. In strong superposition, the model stores broadly everything, but pays a crowding penalty.

📉

Scaling law

As width m grows, average interference falls roughly like 1/m. That creates smooth power-law improvement.

Two regimes, different stories.

The paper distinguishes weak and strong superposition. Real LLM output layers look like the strong version.

loss ∝ 1 / width

Not all loss, of course — just the model-size-dependent component caused by representation crowding. Parsing, data quality, optimization, and architecture still matter.

What the paper actually adds.

The original paper is not just “scaling laws happen because bigger models have more room.” It separates two mechanisms, then checks which one looks like modern LLMs.

The toy model

Data is built from many sparse latent features. Each feature has a frequency pᵢ; the model embeds n features into only m dimensions and reconstructs them. Weight decay is used as a dial: positive decay pushes toward weak/no superposition, while negative decay encourages unit-norm rows and strong superposition.

  • n: number of atomic features.
  • m: representation width, with m ≪ n.
  • ϕ₁/₂: fraction of feature vectors whose norm is meaningfully “on”.

Weak superposition

The model mostly represents the most frequent features and ignores the tail. Loss is dominated by the omitted features, so scaling only becomes a clean power law when the feature frequencies themselves follow a power law.

if pᵢ ∝ i−α, then loss ∝ m−(α−1)

Strong superposition

The model represents far more features than dimensions by letting vectors overlap. For roughly isotropic vectors, squared overlap has mean about 1/m. That geometric fact makes the loss-width curve robust even when the data distribution is not carefully tuned.

  • Important rows become ETF-like: evenly spread with low overlap variance.
  • The relevant error is interference between represented features, not merely dropped features.

LLM evidence

The authors treat output-token rows in the language-model head as feature vectors. Across OPT, GPT-2, Qwen2.5, and Pythia families, normalized row overlaps fall close to 1/m, and the model-size-dependent loss fit gives an exponent near one.

  • Reported LLM fit: αₘ = 0.91 ± 0.04.
  • Chinchilla-derived width exponent: about 0.88 ± 0.06.

More from the original paper.

Pulled closer to the source, this is the paper’s argument in the order you would meet it in the text: definition, controlled toy world, scaling regimes, then the real-LLM check.

1. What “features” mean here

The paper treats inputs as sparse combinations of latent features. A model succeeds when it can assign each active feature a representation vector that can later be decoded. Superposition begins when there are more useful features than available dimensions.

  • Feature frequency pᵢ: how often a feature appears in the data.
  • Activation density E: expected active features per example.
  • Representation loss: error from not cleanly storing/reconstructing those features.

2. The weight-decay dial

A useful experimental trick: the authors change decoupled weight decay to force different representational behaviors. Positive decay penalizes feature-vector norms and encourages dropping low-frequency features. Negative decay encourages many vectors to stay “on,” creating strong superposition.

This lets them vary the degree of superposition without changing the data distribution itself.

3. Why weak scaling is fragile

In weak superposition, the model is basically doing triage: store the common features and ignore the long tail. The loss is the remaining probability mass in that tail. A clean power law appears only if the tail probabilities already have the right shape.

That is the “power law in, power law out” diagnosis.

4. Why strong scaling is robust

In strong superposition, most features are represented, so the dominant failure is not absence — it is interference. In high dimensions, random-ish vectors become nearly orthogonal. The average squared dot product shrinks like 1/m, so increasing width directly reduces overlap noise.

This is the paper’s central mechanistic bridge from geometry to neural scaling.

5. Synthetic distributions tested

The authors test several feature-frequency shapes — power-law, exponential, and linear-like distributions. Weak superposition only gives power-law loss when the data distribution cooperates. Strong superposition gives inverse-width scaling much more generally.

6. What they measure in real LLMs

They inspect rows of the unembedding / language-model head as token representation vectors. The key measurement is how normalized overlaps between rows shrink with model dimension. Across GPT-2, OPT, Pythia, and Qwen2.5 families, the slope is close to the strong-superposition prediction.

7. Chinchilla connection

The paper compares this width-based mechanism with Chinchilla-style scaling laws. The reported Chinchilla-derived width exponent is close to one, consistent with overlap-limited representation loss.

8. Where the spell breaks

The authors expect this mechanism to stop dominating once representation width is large enough to allocate clean directions to the relevant feature set. After that, other bottlenecks — data, depth, optimization, parsing/computation — should become the main battlefield.

So what follows?

✅

Scaling has a mechanism

It is not magic. If loss comes partly from vector interference, adding dimensions has a predictable payoff.

🏁

There is a ceiling

Once width is comparable to vocabulary/features, overlap-driven gains should diminish. Other bottlenecks take over.

🔍

Interpretability gets harder

Dense superposition improves packing efficiency, but overlapping concepts make circuits and features less clean to trace.

How scaling might improve

The paper is cautious: natural language probably cannot beat the power law just by changing width, but very skewed domain tasks might. Training or architecture choices that encourage useful superposition may lower constants — smaller models behaving like larger ones — even if the exponent does not change.

What this does not explain

The analysis focuses on representation loss, especially width. It does not fully solve parsing loss in transformer layers, dataset-size scaling, training-step scaling, or why LLMs land in strong superposition. Those are explicitly left as open directions.
